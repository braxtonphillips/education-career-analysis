The Education and Career Success dataset our group chose provides a thorough view of student profiles through a variety of attribute types. The categorical variables can be further classified into nominal and ordinal data. Nominal variables include Student_ID serving as a unique identifier with no inherent order, Gender with three distinct categories, Field_of_Study spanning seven academic disciplines, and Entrepreneurship as a binary variable. Current_Job_Level represents an ordinal variable as it has a clear hierarchical progression from Entry to Mid to Senior to Executive. The numerical variables encompass both interval and ratio measurements. Interval variables include rating scales such as Soft_Skills_Score, Networking_Score, Career_Satisfaction, and Work_Life_Balance, all measured on scales from 1-10 where the intervals between values are meaningful but lack a true zero. Ratio variables include Age, High_School_GPA and University_GPA, SAT_Score, University_Ranking, and quantitative counts like Internships_Completed, Projects_Completed, Certifications, and Job_Offers. Starting_Salary is a ratio variable allowing for meaningful proportional comparisons.
Key features of this qualitative-quantitative mixed dataset include its impressive completeness, with zero missing values across all 20 variables for all 5,000 records. The gender distribution shows approximately 49% male and 47% female representation. The dataset captures a young professional demographic with ages ranging from 18-29 years. Nearly half of individuals are at entry-level positions, while only about 20% have pursued entrepreneurship. The dataset's balanced representation across academic disciplines provides an excellent foundation for comparative analysis. Particularly noteworthy is the surprising lack of strong correlations between variables, suggesting that career outcomes may depend on complex, non-linear interactions rather than simple relationships between individual factors.
